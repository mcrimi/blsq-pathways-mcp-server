# Walk in Her Shoes: Northern Nigeria
## Pathways Qualitative Context Resource

> Use this resource to ground vulnerability analysis in the lived realities of women in Northern Nigeria. These are not case studies — they are structural portraits. Always read alongside `lens.md`.

---

## Geographic & Cultural Context

**Locations:** Kano State (Warawa, Sharada communities), Kaduna State (Rimingado community)  
**Setting:** Rural and peri-urban communities in North West Nigeria  
**Key structural features:**
- Deep patriarchal and Islamic gender norms governing women's movement, decision-making, and public life
- Polygamous household structures are common and normative — co-wife dynamics shape daily resource allocation, emotional support, and health access
- Male migration (to Lagos and other cities) creates absent-partner households where women bear full domestic and childcare burdens while financial control remains with husbands
- Purdah (female seclusion) limits mobility; women require husband's permission to leave the compound, including for healthcare
- Children are valued as religious blessings; large family size is aspirational for both men and women — this frames contraception as culturally suspect
- Chemists are the first point of care for most families; hospitals are distant, costly, and trusted less than informal providers

## Women's Portraits

### Hassana, 32 — Warawa, Kano State

**Household:** Fifth of 18 children from her father's household. Married at 14 to a man twice her age who now has four wives. Five surviving children; lost one infant at 3 months. Husband earns irregular income selling vegetables; Hassana supplements with knitting.

**Vulnerability pathway:**

| Domain | Key Factors |
|--------|-------------|
| Personal History | No formal schooling; religious school only. Father did not educate daughters. Taught domestic labor from childhood. No knowledge of menstruation until it arrived. |
| Household Dynamics | Married at 14 to man twice her age — no say in marriage. Polygamous household with co-wife tension. Husband "disciplines" her; she frames this as his right. Low emotional support. |
| Economic Stability | Husband's income is unstable and insufficient for four families. Knitting provides irregular supplemental income. Food insecurity is frequent. |
| Social Capital | Limited social network; cannot leave without husband's permission. Community norms enforce female seclusion. |
| Structural Environment | Healthcare access requires husband's permission. Chemist preferred over hospital — closer, cheaper. No transport. |
| Biological Health | 7 pregnancies; lost second child at 3 months; lost seventh child at birth due to complications. 5 of 7 births at home. Does not use family planning. |

**Friction points in Journey of Care:**
- Husband controls access to healthcare — she cannot go to a facility without his permission
- Preference for home delivery reflects community norm, not individual choice
- No family planning use; children framed as Allah's blessing; husband alignment with large family norm
- Chemist-first care pathway delays diagnosis and treatment of serious conditions

**What she wants:** To provide adequately for her children. She expresses loneliness and wishes for more social connection, but accepts the structure as her life. No expressed desire to change household power dynamics.

**What constrains her:** Absolute dependency on husband's permission and income. Internalized gender norms. Structural isolation. No education to navigate health systems independently.

### Zainab, 38 — Sharada, Kano State

**Household:** Orphaned at 5 (maternal death from chronic illness). Raised by grandmother (died when Zainab was 12), then by uncle. Left school before completion. Married at 14 to a 35-year-old man. Now primary earner: husband's sight is failing and he no longer works. Eight living children; one stillbirth (first pregnancy after prolonged labor at home); two high-risk deliveries with severe bleeding.

**Vulnerability pathway:**

| Domain | Key Factors |
|--------|-------------|
| Personal History | Double orphaning (mother at 5, grandmother at 12) — no stable adult attachment. Removed from school at 12. No family of origin to return to. Entirely dependent on husband's household. |
| Household Dynamics | Married at 14 to man 21 years her senior. Co-wife recently added (age 16). Husband no longer earning — Zainab now sole income earner while retaining full domestic responsibility. Low emotional support. |
| Economic Stability | Sells bean pudding; children sell charcoal and firewood. Income is precarious. Household has two rooms, no toilet. One episode of near-fatal malaria in a child required borrowing money for treatment — debt still unresolved. |
| Social Capital | Has a trusted community friend who shares health information. Attends naming ceremonies and weddings. Strong informal mutual aid network ("when one is struggling, we all pitch in"). |
| Structural Environment | Hospital access was blocked by family norms in first pregnancy — stillbirth resulted. Subsequent births mixed (hospital and home). No TV/radio, limiting health information access. |
| Biological Health | 10 pregnancies; 8 living children; 1 stillbirth; 2 high-risk deliveries with hemorrhage. Body exhausted. Now considering family planning for the first time. |

**Friction points in Journey of Care:**
- First stillbirth directly caused by delayed hospital transfer after prolonged home labor — family norm overrode her clinical need
- Near-fatal child illness treated at chemist first; by the time hospital was reached, blood transfusion was needed
- Financial barrier is now primary: cannot afford hospital care without debt
- Family planning interest is emerging but requires husband's buy-in she has not yet sought

**Intergenerational pattern:** Orphaned at 5 → no education → early marriage → sole economic provider in aging household → considering family planning only after physical exhaustion

**Bright spot:** Strong community social capital. Her friendship network is a real asset for information and mutual support.

### Balkis, 24 — Rimingado, Kaduna State

**Household:** Third of 8 children; father monogamous and education-supportive. Completed both primary and secondary school — unusual for her context. Arranged marriage at 18 to a 38-year-old trader who travels to Lagos 3 months per year. Polygamous household (third wife). Three children.

**Vulnerability pathway:**

| Domain | Key Factors |
|--------|-------------|
| Personal History | Comparatively advantaged: completed secondary education, stable childhood, father valued daughters' schooling. Enters adulthood with more knowledge and agency than most. |
| Household Dynamics | 20-year age gap with husband; traditional husband who controls her mobility. Co-wives present — initially distant, cooperative over time. Migrant husband: away 3 months/year. |
| Economic Stability | Husband sends remittances; Balkis does not earn. Rising prices making household management harder. No independent income. |
| Social Capital | Closest confidant is husband's elder brother's wife. Attends community events; hears about family planning through peer networks. Moderate social integration. |
| Structural Environment | Husband permits healthcare visits — this is a genuine advantage over Hassana's situation. Rural location but access is not the primary barrier. |
| Biological Health | 3 children. Attended ANC and delivered in hospital with support. Interested in family planning but husband opposed (fears side effects, future infertility). Last pregnancy was medically complicated — doctor advised spacing. |

**Friction points in Journey of Care:**
- Family planning: she wants it, has the knowledge, and the medical rationale — but cannot act without husband's agreement
- Internalized relational calculus: "If I insist, it might cause a dispute." She is silencing her own informed preference to preserve household stability
- Husband's misinformation about contraception (side effects, infertility) is the operative barrier — not her beliefs

**What makes Balkis's case analytically important:** She demonstrates that *education and healthcare access do not automatically translate to agency*. With a supportive father and secondary school completion, she has more knowledge and fewer structural barriers than Hassana or Zainab. Yet the same household power dynamics — husband control over reproductive decisions, inability to act on her own preferences — constrain her health outcomes. The vulnerability here is concentrated in the Household Dynamics domain, not education or access.

## Cross-Cutting Patterns: Northern Nigeria

### 1. Male gatekeeping is the primary proximate barrier
Across all three women, the husband's permission is required for healthcare access, family planning use, and social mobility. This is not a personal preference — it is structurally enforced by purdah norms, household economics (dependency), and religious framing. Interventions that target women alone without engaging male gatekeepers will have limited reach.

### 2. Religion and family planning are in negotiation, not opposition
Hassana accepts large family size as divine blessing. Balkis and Zainab are both considering or interested in contraception but navigate it through a religious lens (spacing, not limiting). The opening is there — community health workers who frame family planning as compatible with Islamic teaching (birth spacing for mother and child health) have traction with Zainab. The entry point is *spacing*, not *limiting*.

### 3. The chemist-first pathway is a structural risk
All three women's households use the chemist as first point of care. This is a rational economic and logistical decision, but it creates dangerous delays for emergencies (Zainab's daughter's malaria) and normal deliveries (Hassana's seventh child). Programs that ignore the chemist as a node in the care pathway are missing the actual point of first contact.

### 4. Traditional birth attendance and home delivery are deeply normative
Home delivery is the default across all three women, supported by community norms, mobility restrictions, and mistrust of hospital procedures. Zainab's first stillbirth and Hassana's infant deaths are partially attributable to home delivery complications — yet facility delivery is not straightforwardly "better" if it is inaccessible, unaffordable, or experienced as unsafe. The question is not *facility vs home* but *what makes this woman's birth safer in her context*.

### 5. Polygamous household dynamics create specific vulnerability clusters
Resource competition between co-wives and their children (Hassana's quiet food portioning, Zainab's new younger co-wife adding to household burden) generates chronic low-grade stress and insecurity. When a husband's income is unstable, each co-wife's children are in structural competition. This is not interpersonal conflict — it is resource scarcity expressed through household structure.

### 6. Education creates knowledge but not autonomy
Balkis's case is a caution against assuming that education solves the problem. Her knowledge of ANC, contraception, and her own medical needs is clear. Her autonomy to act on that knowledge is almost as constrained as Zainab's. Structural change in household power dynamics — not information transfer alone — is required.

## Intervention Implications

**Engage husbands as the primary decision-maker for healthcare access**
Designing programs that improve women's health knowledge without creating pathways for husbands to support or permit healthcare-seeking will plateau. Male engagement programming in Northern Nigeria must address the actual locus of decision-making.

**Use Islamic framing for family planning messaging**
"Birth spacing for the health of mother and child is not against our religion" — this message, delivered by a trusted community health worker in Zainab's story, created genuine openness. Religious authority figures and community health workers who frame family planning within Islamic ethics are the most credible messengers.

**Invest in the chemist as a care node**
Rather than bypassing the chemist, programs should consider equipping chemists to recognize danger signs and facilitate appropriate referrals. The chemist is where these households first turn — that is where triage actually happens.

**Support women who are sole earners**
Zainab is the primary income earner for a large household, yet retains full domestic and childcare responsibilities and has no economic safety net. Economic empowerment programs designed for women who are *already* earning — not just women seeking entry into income generation — are relevant here.

**Home delivery safety as a realistic near-term goal**
For segments with strong home birth preference and real mobility barriers, improving the safety of home deliveries (trained TBAs, clean delivery kits, clear escalation pathways) may save more lives in the near term than campaigns to shift preference toward facility delivery.

**Address childhood deprivation as a precursor**
Zainab's double orphaning and interrupted education set the conditions for everything that followed. Hassana's complete absence of schooling similarly foreclosed options. Investment in girls' education and orphan protection in Northern Nigeria has long-term maternal and child health returns.

## Analyst Notes

- Hassana and Zainab represent the more constrained end of the Northern Nigeria vulnerability spectrum: low education, high birth parity, inadequate healthcare access, and limited economic autonomy
- Balkis represents a segment with *relatively* better resources but demonstrates how household power dynamics can neutralize educational advantages
- All three share the structural features of purdah, male gatekeeping, and polygamy — these are not personal circumstances but systemic features of their context
- Northern Nigeria shares the absent-partner dynamic with Bihar, India, but the mechanism differs: in Bihar, partners migrate for economic necessity; in Northern Nigeria, mobility and trade patterns drive absence while purdah prevents women from filling the gap through public-sphere activity
- The religious dimension is more operationally central in Northern Nigeria than in Kenya or Bihar — it shapes menstrual knowledge, family planning acceptability, gender role framing, and healthcare trust simultaneously
