# Walk in Her Shoes: Northern Nigeria

> Use this resource to ground vulnerability analysis in the lived realities of women in Northern Nigeria. These are not case studies — they are structural portraits. Always read alongside `lens.md`.

---

## Geographic & Cultural Context

**Locations:** Kano State (Warawa, Sharada communities), Kaduna State (Rimingado community)
**Setting:** Rural and peri-urban communities in North West Nigeria

**Key structural features:**
- Deep patriarchal and Islamic gender norms governing women's movement, decision-making, and public life
- Polygamous household structures are common and normative — co-wife dynamics shape daily resource allocation, emotional support, and health access
- Male migration (to Lagos and other cities) creates absent-partner households where women bear full domestic and childcare burdens while financial control remains with husbands
- Purdah (female seclusion) limits mobility; women require husband's permission to leave the compound, including for healthcare
- Children are valued as religious blessings; large family size is aspirational for both men and women — this frames contraception as culturally suspect
- Chemists are the first point of care for most families; hospitals are distant, costly, and trusted less than informal providers

## Women's Portraits

### Hassana, 32 — Warawa, Kano State

**Household:** Fifth of 18 children from her father's household. Married at 14 to a man twice her age who now has four wives. Five surviving children; lost one infant at 3 months. Husband earns irregular income selling vegetables; Hassana supplements with knitting.

**Vulnerability pathway:**

| Domain | Key factors |
|---|---|
| Woman & Her Past Experiences | No formal schooling; religious school only. Father did not educate daughters. Taught domestic labor from childhood. No knowledge of menstruation until it arrived. |
| Health Mental Models | Children framed as Allah's blessing; large family normative. Chemist trusted over hospital. |
| Household Relationships | Married at 14 to man twice her age — no say in marriage. Polygamous household with co-wife tension. Husband "disciplines" her; she frames this as his right. Low emotional support. |
| Household Economics & Living Conditions | Husband's income unstable and insufficient for four families. Knitting provides irregular supplemental income. Food insecurity is frequent. |
| Social Support | Limited social network; cannot leave without husband's permission. Community norms enforce female seclusion. |
| Human & Natural Systems | Healthcare access requires husband's permission. Chemist preferred over hospital — closer, cheaper. No transport. |

**Friction points in Journey of Care:**
- Husband controls access to healthcare — she cannot go to a facility without his permission
- Preference for home delivery reflects community norm, not individual choice
- No family planning use; children framed as Allah's blessing; husband aligned with large family norm
- Chemist-first care pathway delays diagnosis and treatment of serious conditions

**What she wants:** To provide adequately for her children. She expresses loneliness and wishes for more social connection, but accepts the structure as her life.

**What constrains her:** Absolute dependency on husband's permission and income. Internalized gender norms. Structural isolation. No education to navigate health systems independently.

### Zainab, 38 — Sharada, Kano State

**Household:** Orphaned at 5 (maternal death from chronic illness). Raised by grandmother (died when Zainab was 12), then by uncle. Left school before completion. Married at 14 to a 35-year-old man. Now primary earner: husband's sight is failing and he no longer works. Eight living children; one stillbirth (first pregnancy after prolonged labor at home); two high-risk deliveries with severe bleeding.

**Vulnerability pathway:**

| Domain | Key factors |
|---|---|
| Woman & Her Past Experiences | Double orphaning (mother at 5, grandmother at 12) — no stable adult attachment. Removed from school at 12. No family of origin to return to. Entirely dependent on husband's household. |
| Health Mental Models | Chemist-first for child illness; hospital as last resort. Family planning interest is emerging after physical exhaustion. |
| Household Relationships | Married at 14 to man 21 years her senior. Co-wife recently added (age 16). Husband no longer earning — Zainab now sole income earner while retaining full domestic responsibility. Low emotional support. |
| Household Economics & Living Conditions | Sells bean pudding; children sell charcoal and firewood. Income precarious. Two rooms, no toilet. Debt from near-fatal child malaria unresolved. |
| Social Support | Trusted community friend who shares health information. Attends naming ceremonies and weddings. Strong informal mutual aid network. |
| Human & Natural Systems | Hospital access was blocked by family norms in first pregnancy — stillbirth resulted. Subsequent births mixed (hospital and home). No TV/radio, limiting health information access. |

**Friction points in Journey of Care:**
- First stillbirth directly caused by delayed hospital transfer after prolonged home labor — family norm overrode her clinical need
- Near-fatal child illness treated at chemist first; by the time hospital was reached, blood transfusion was needed
- Financial barrier now primary: cannot afford hospital care without debt
- Family planning interest is emerging but requires husband's buy-in she has not yet sought

**Intergenerational pattern:** Orphaned at 5 → no education → early marriage → sole economic provider in aging household → considering family planning only after physical exhaustion.

**Bright spot:** Strong community social capital. Her friendship network is a real asset for information and mutual support.

### Balkis, 24 — Rimingado, Kaduna State

**Household:** Third of 8 children; father monogamous and education-supportive. Completed both primary and secondary school — unusual for her context. Arranged marriage at 18 to a 38-year-old trader who travels to Lagos 3 months per year. Polygamous household (third wife). Three children.

**Vulnerability pathway:**

| Domain | Key factors |
|---|---|
| Woman & Her Past Experiences | Comparatively advantaged: completed secondary education, stable childhood, father valued daughters' schooling. Enters adulthood with more knowledge and agency than most. |
| Health Mental Models | Informed about ANC and contraception; understands medical rationale for spacing. Husband holds misinformation (fears side effects, infertility). |
| Household Relationships | 20-year age gap; traditional husband who controls her mobility. Co-wives — initially distant, cooperative over time. Migrant husband: away 3 months/year. |
| Household Economics & Living Conditions | Husband sends remittances; Balkis does not earn. Rising prices making household management harder. No independent income. |
| Social Support | Closest confidant is husband's elder brother's wife. Attends community events; hears about family planning through peer networks. Moderate integration. |
| Human & Natural Systems | Husband permits healthcare visits — a genuine advantage. Rural location but access is not the primary barrier. |

**Friction points in Journey of Care:**
- Family planning: she wants it, has the knowledge, and the medical rationale — but cannot act without husband's agreement
- Internalized relational calculus: "If I insist, it might cause a dispute." She silences her own informed preference to preserve household stability
- Husband's misinformation about contraception is the operative barrier — not her beliefs
