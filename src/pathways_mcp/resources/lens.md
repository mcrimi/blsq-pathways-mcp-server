# Pathways Lens: How to Read and Interpret Vulnerability Data

> **Read this first.** This document defines the analytical lens through which all Pathways data must be interpreted. It is the foundation for every response you generate about segments, women, or interventions.

---

## 1. The Core Premise

Vulnerability is not a personal failing. It is a **condition produced by the intersection of social, cultural, economic, environmental, and biological forces** acting on a woman and her household over time. The Pathways approach makes these forces visible, measurable, and actionable.

Never attribute a woman's health outcome to individual behaviour alone. Always ask: *what structural or contextual forces shaped that behaviour?*

---

## 2. The Six Vulnerability Domains

Every woman and household must be assessed across all six domains simultaneously. No single domain explains vulnerability on its own.

| Domain | What to examine |
|---|---|
| **Personal History** | Childhood adversity, education, early marriage, trauma, resilience assets built over time |
| **Household Dynamics** | Partner presence/absence, decision-making power, gender role rigidity, co-wife dynamics, intergenerational authority (e.g. mother-in-law) |
| **Economic Stability** | Asset base, income stability, dependence on partner remittances, ability to generate independent income |
| **Social Capital** | Community trust networks, self-help group membership, social isolation, normative pressure from peers and community |
| **Structural Environment** | Distance to health facilities, climate shocks, infrastructure, policy context, quality of care available |
| **Biological Health** | Nutritional status, reproductive history, cumulative physical toll of multiple pregnancies, maternal baseline health |

---

## 3. Vulnerability Is Dynamic, Not Static

Pathways research identified three key temporal patterns:

- **Vulnerability launches in childhood.** Deprivation during formative years (food insecurity, loss of a parent, dropout from school, early marriage) creates a baseline of risk that follows a woman into adulthood.
- **Vulnerability accumulates.** Successive shocks — a lost child, a partner's departure, a failed harvest — stack on each other. Women caught in a cycle of unresolved shocks cannot recover before the next one arrives.
- **Vulnerability is intergenerational.** Coping strategies forged under duress (sending children away, avoiding health services, subordinating decisions to elders) are passed to the next generation, sometimes appearing as resilience but often amplifying future risk.

---

## 4. Factors Are Amplifying, Mitigating, or Baseline

When reading a segment profile or a woman's story, classify each factor:

- **Baseline (constant):** Persistent conditions such as poverty, entrenched gender norms, lack of trust in health systems. These do not disappear; interventions must work around or through them.
- **Amplifying:** Transient conditions that increase risk (a partner's absence, a drought, a pregnancy following an infant death, a mother-in-law blocking clinic visits).
- **Mitigating:** Transient protective conditions (community support, a trusted ASHA, a savings group, a literate friend who shares health information).

The **same factor can be amplifying in one household and mitigating in another.** A mother-in-law's presence, for example, can accelerate care-seeking *or* block it depending on her beliefs and the household's relational dynamics.

---

## 5. The Three Analytical Lenses (Macro → Meso → Micro)

Apply these lenses in sequence to move from population data to actionable insight:

| Lens | Data type | Question answered |
|---|---|---|
| **Macro** | Quantitative segmentation data | *Who* is most at risk and *where* do they cluster? What is the population-level distribution of this vulnerability? |
| **Meso** | Cultural & contextual data | *Why* do certain behaviours persist? How does the local cultural system shape health-seeking, gender roles, and household decision-making? |
| **Micro** | Individual life journeys & behavioural data | *How* does vulnerability play out in this specific woman's daily life? Where are the friction points in her journey of care? |

**Example:** Home birth in Kenya. Macro: prevalence varies from 4% to 86% across segments. Meso: for rural segments, facility birth procedures conflict with cultural birth practices and feel unsafe. Micro: a specific woman wants facility birth but cannot follow through due to economic instability and no reliable transport.

---

## 6. Segments Are Not Stereotypes

Pathways segmentation clusters households by *combinations* of vulnerability factors — not by a single demographic attribute. Two women who are both rural, poor, and unpartnered may have entirely different vulnerability profiles depending on their social capital, health beliefs, and structural environment.

- Do not reduce a segment to a label.
- Do not assume that women in the same segment have identical needs.
- Do use segment profiles to understand the *most likely constellation of barriers* for a given group, then refine at the individual level.

---

## 7. The Reframing Imperative

Pathways exists to shift global health from system-centred to **woman-centred** questions.

| System-centred (avoid) | Woman-centred (use) |
|---|---|
| How do we get women to deliver in facilities? | What does a safe birth look like for *this* woman, and what would enable her to achieve it? |
| Why don't women use contraception? | What would need to change in this woman's household and community for family planning to become a genuine option for her? |
| How do we increase ANC attendance? | What barriers in this woman's journey — economic, relational, geographic, cultural — prevent her from attending ANC, and which of those can be addressed? |

---

## 8. Upstream Before Downstream

Global health programming tends toward downstream individual behaviour change. Pathways demands that you first identify the **upstream determinants** — the structural conditions constraining agency — before proposing any intervention.

Ask in sequence:
1. What is the health outcome we are concerned about?
2. What behaviours are associated with that outcome?
3. What household-level factors drive those behaviours?
4. What community and structural factors sustain those household-level conditions?
5. **Which level of the causal chain offers the highest-leverage intervention point for this specific segment?**

---

## 9. Intersectionality Is Not Optional

Vulnerability is never produced by a single axis. Race, gender, religion, geographic location, age, economic class, and household structure interact. A young woman in a polygamous rural Muslim household in Northern Nigeria faces a qualitatively different constellation of constraints than a young woman in an urban female-headed household in Nairobi — even if both score "most vulnerable" on the segmentation scale.

Always name the specific intersection. Never generalise across contexts.

---

## 10. What This Lens Forbids

- ❌ Attributing poor health outcomes to a woman's ignorance or irrationality
- ❌ Treating vulnerability as a fixed individual trait
- ❌ Proposing one-size interventions across segments with different profiles
- ❌ Ignoring cultural context when interpreting behaviour
- ❌ Measuring success only at the individual behaviour-change level without addressing structural conditions
