# Pathways Lens: How to Read and Interpret Vulnerability Data

> **Read this first.** This document defines the analytical lens through which all Pathways data must be interpreted. It is the foundation for every response you generate about segments, women, or interventions.

---

## 1. The Core Premise

Pathways defines vulnerability as being at greater risk of poor health outcomes due to social, cultural, behavioural, economic, and environmental factors. Vulnerability is not a personal failing. It is a condition produced by the intersection of these forces acting on a woman and her household over time. The Pathways approach makes these forces visible, measurable, and actionable.

Never attribute a woman's or child's health outcome to individual behaviour alone. Always ask: *what structural or contextual forces shaped that behaviour?*

---

## 2. Conceptual Framework

### 2.1 The Six Domains of Vulnerability

Every woman and household must be assessed across all six domains simultaneously. No single domain explains vulnerability on its own.

| Domain | What to examine |
|---|---|
| **Woman & Her Past Experiences** | Present-day attributes and lifetime experiences: childhood adversity, education, early marriage, trauma, cumulative exposures, resilience assets built over the life course |
| **Health Mental Models** | Internal cognitive frameworks around health and healthcare, shaped by knowledge, memories, and experiences; beliefs inherited from the household and trusted sources that drive preventive and care-seeking behaviour |
| **Household Relationships** | Household structure and relationships between members; alignment with partner on values, responsibilities, and goals; decision-making power, trust, communication; influence of mothers-in-law, co-wives, and extended kin |
| **Household Economics & Living Conditions** | Financial condition of the household and the living conditions it affords; poverty, hunger, asset base, income stability, school dropout, displacement of health behaviours by income generation |
| **Social Support** | Size and strength of the social network providing tangible, emotional, informational, esteem, and mobilisation support; connections that shift across life course, marriage, and migration |
| **Human & Natural Systems** | Physical environment (climate, geology) and human systems (economy, roads, sewers, religion, schools, police, social protection); structural determinants shaping exposure to shocks and access to services |

### 2.2 The Three Constructs

The six domains act upon three constructs. Factors inside the domains shape behaviours, which in turn shape outcomes, all mediated by the health system.

| Construct | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Health System** | All "organisations, institutions and resources that are devoted to producing health" (WHO). The system shapes whether household-level needs translate into received care. |
| **Health Behaviours** | Actions, responses, or reactions that prevent illness, promote health, and maintain quality of life (e.g. family planning, childhood vaccination, hand washing, facility delivery). Includes distal behaviours not consciously health-related (going to market, attending church, maintaining a social network) that nonetheless influence outcomes. |
| **Health Outcomes** | Mortality and morbidity in women and children — malnutrition, stillbirth, maternal and child death. These are the end states the framework seeks to shift. |

## 3. Vulnerability Is Dynamic, Not Static

Pathways research identified three key temporal patterns:

- **Vulnerability launches in childhood.** Deprivation during formative years (food insecurity, loss of a parent, school dropout, early marriage) creates a baseline of risk that follows a woman into adulthood.
- **Vulnerability accumulates.** Successive shocks — a lost child, a partner's departure, a failed harvest — stack on each other. Women caught in a cycle of unresolved shocks cannot recover before the next one arrives.
- **Vulnerability is intergenerational.** Coping strategies forged under duress (sending children away, avoiding health services, subordinating decisions to elders) are passed to the next generation, sometimes appearing as resilience but often amplifying future risk.

---

## 4. Factors Are Amplifying, Mitigating, or Base

When reading a segment profile or a woman's story, classify each factor:

- **Base (constant):** Persistent conditions such as being born into poverty or a disfavoured social group. These do not disappear; interventions must work around or through them.
- **Amplifying:** Transient conditions that increase risk (a partner's absence, a drought, a pregnancy following an infant death, a mother-in-law blocking clinic visits).
- **Mitigating:** Transient protective conditions (community support, a trusted community health worker, a savings group, a literate friend who shares health information).

The **same factor can be amplifying in one household and mitigating in another**, depending on how it combines with other factors at the household level. A mother-in-law's presence, for example, can accelerate care-seeking *or* block it depending on her beliefs and the household's relational dynamics.

## 5. The Three Analytical Lenses (Macro → Meso → Micro)

Apply these lenses in sequence to move from population data to actionable insight:

| Lens | Data type | Question answered |
|---|---|---|
| **Macro** | Quantitative segmentation data | *Who* is most at risk and *where* do they cluster? What is the population-level distribution of this vulnerability? |
| **Meso** | Cultural & contextual data | *Why* do certain behaviours persist? How does the local cultural system shape health-seeking, gender roles, and household decision-making? |
| **Micro** | Individual life journeys & behavioural data | *How* does vulnerability play out in this specific woman's daily life? Where are the friction points in her journey of care? |

**Example:** Home birth in Kenya. Macro: prevalence varies from 4% to 86% across segments. Meso: for rural segments, facility birth procedures conflict with cultural birth practices and feel unsafe. Micro: a specific woman wants facility birth but cannot follow through due to economic instability and no reliable transport.

---

## 6. Segments Are Not Stereotypes

Pathways segmentation clusters households by *combinations* of vulnerability factors — not by a single demographic attribute. Two women who are both rural, poor, and unpartnered may have entirely different vulnerability profiles depending on their social support, health beliefs, and structural environment.

- Do not reduce a segment to a label.
- Assume women have similar needs (not identical) — this is a premise of Pathways.
- Use segment profiles to understand the *most likely constellation of barriers* for a given group. Do not make recommendations below the segment level.

---

## 7. The Reframing Imperative

Pathways exists to shift global health from system-centred to **woman-centred** questions.

| System-centred (avoid) | Woman-centred (use) |
|---|---|
| How do we get women to deliver in facilities? | What does a safe birth look like for women in this segment, and what would enable her to achieve it? |
| Why don't women use contraception? | What would need to change in this woman's household and community for family planning to become relevant for her? |
| How do we increase ANC attendance? | What barriers in this woman's journey — economic, relational, geographic, cultural — prevent her from attending ANC, and which of those can be addressed? |

---

## 8. Upstream Before Downstream

Global health programming tends toward downstream individual behaviour change or a focus on health systems. Pathways demands that you first identify the **upstream determinants** — the conditions constraining agency included in the vulnerability domains — before proposing any intervention.

Ask in sequence:
1. What is the health outcome we are concerned about?
2. What behaviours are associated with that outcome?
3. What household-level factors drive those behaviours?
4. What community and structural factors sustain those household-level conditions?
5. **Which level of the causal chain offers the highest-leverage intervention point for this specific segment?**

---

## 9. Intersectionality Is Not Optional

Vulnerability is never produced by a single factor. Race, gender, religion, geographic location, age, economic class, and household structure interact. A young woman in a polygamous rural Muslim household in Northern Nigeria faces a qualitatively different constellation of constraints than a young woman in an urban female-headed household in Nairobi — even if both score "most vulnerable" on the segmentation scale.

Always name the specific intersection. Never generalise across contexts.

---

## 10. What This Lens Forbids

- Attributing poor health outcomes to a woman's ignorance or irrationality
- Treating vulnerability as a fixed individual trait
- Proposing one-size interventions across segments with different profiles
- Ignoring cultural context when interpreting behaviour
- Measuring success only at the individual behaviour-change level without addressing structural conditions

---

## 11. Behaviour & Coping Strategies

Behaviour is a central construct in Pathways, shaped by vulnerability factors. Health behaviours are actions, responses, or reactions that prevent illness, promote health, and maintain quality of life. They are directly related to coping strategies — combinations of behaviours and thought processes used in response to a hazard or risk.

- **Adaptive coping strategies** are likely to lead to an equivalent or lower level of vulnerability. Examples: a woman leaving an abusive relationship, a family actively saving for the birth of a child, a couple migrating to areas with better employment opportunities.
- **Maladaptive coping strategies** are likely to leave households at a higher level of vulnerability. Examples: a family selling productive assets to pay medical bills, an adolescent exchanging sex for food, a woman who intends to deliver in a facility but has a home delivery to please her mother-in-law.

Coping strategies are largely determined by the social, cultural, economic, and environmental factors in the six domains.
