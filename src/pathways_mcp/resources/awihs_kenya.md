# Walk in Her Shoes: Kenya

> This document synthesises the qualitative life stories collected in Kenya as part of the Pathways "Walk in Her Shoes" series. Kenya's stories span contrasting geographies and cultures — nomadic pastoralist communities in Tana River and Turkana counties, and urban informal settlements in Nairobi — illuminating how vulnerability manifests differently across structural environments while sharing common upstream drivers.

---

## Context

Kenya's Pathways research captured stories from three distinct settings:

- **Tana River County (Wardey):** Remote, predominantly Somali-Kenyan, Muslim, pastoralist community. Climate shocks from drought and flood cycles. Female genital mutilation/cutting (FGM/C) practiced. Early marriage normative. Health facilities distant; traditional birth attendants (mkunga) central to maternity care.
- **Turkana County:** Semi-arid, nomadic/semi-nomadic pastoralist context. Extreme food insecurity and water scarcity. Climate change is actively reducing household viability. Husband absent for most of the year following livestock. Very limited health infrastructure.
- **Nairobi — Mathare informal settlement:** Dense urban poverty. Female-headed households common. Women in informal labour (fish selling, house cleaning). High exposure to violence and insecurity. Fragmented social networks; significant individual resilience.

The Kenya segmentation identified **9 segments** ranging from least to most vulnerable, cutting across rural/urban and male/female-headed household lines. The most vulnerable segments (UF4-K and RM4-K) illustrate radically different vulnerability profiles despite similar vulnerability scores.

## The Women

### Amina, 19 — Wardey, Tana River County

**Household:** Third wife in a polygamous marriage; two children; husband is livestock herder and sole provider; lives in extended family compound with co-wives, mother-in-law, brothers-in-law.

**Life arc:** Born into a nomadic family; never attended formal school; attended madrasa (Quranic school). Circumcised at age 10. Periods began but she received no accurate menstrual health information — a community belief held that sanitary pads cause cancer. At 16, father found a husband more than twice her age; she was frightened but had no voice in the decision. Now a third wife in a large household structured around clearly defined gender roles. Has had three pregnancies; second child was a stillbirth at home. Third delivery required a 5-hour walk in active labour at night to a clinic where no doctor was present — then an emergency car transfer to the nearest town. Has attended ANC only once across three pregnancies, by chance when an outreach team visited her area. She worried her husband would find out she had attended without asking his permission. Dreams of having ten children. Describes the 40-day postpartum rest as the only time in her life she is cared for by others.

**Key vulnerability drivers:** No formal education → FGM/C → very early marriage with extreme age gap → no decision-making power over health → structural inaccessibility of facilities → climate-driven economic deterioration → traditional birth practices incompatible with facility care.

**Mitigating factors:** Co-wife solidarity in day-to-day work. Community of women at home who support her through labour. Husband provides food and medicine within his means.

**Friction points in care:** Cannot attend health services without husband's permission; facility is a day's walk; outreach services are rare and unscheduled; ANC attendance itself is an act of secrecy; facility birth procedures conflict with cultural postpartum practices (40-day rest indoors).

### Maggy, 30 — Mathare, Nairobi

**Household:** Female-headed; provider and decision-maker; three children (biological son and daughter, adopted niece whose mother died); younger sister living with her, currently pregnant.

**Life arc:** Grew up in a rural town; dropped out of school when her father died to care for siblings. At 16, moved to Nairobi to find work after crop failure; found herself in an informal settlement, cleaning houses and washing clothes, enduring sexual harassment by clients because she needed the income. First pregnancy at 17 after contraceptive injection lapse; partner denied responsibility and disappeared. Second pregnancy from a subsequent relationship — that partner also left when she disclosed the pregnancy. Adopted her sister's orphaned child when the sister died. Now sells fish on a busy highway, leaving pre-dawn, placing children in daycare for 30 shillings a day. Son has a persistent cough she cannot afford to treat. Sends money to an ageing, ill mother in the village. Feels trapped in Mathare because it is the only place she can find any work.

**Key vulnerability drivers:** Parental loss → education truncated → economic migration → urban poverty and insecurity → contraceptive discontinuation leading to unintended pregnancies → serial partner abandonment → compounded caring responsibilities → economic barrier to health access.

**Mitigating factors:** Strong personal agency and resilience. Sister (Mercy) present and supportive. Has health knowledge and navigated facility delivery in second pregnancy. Adopted niece reflects deep relational commitment.

**Friction points in care:** Cannot afford medication for child's illness. Daycare cost competes with health expenditure. Long working hours leave no time for scheduled clinic attendance. Night-time safety prevents accessing nearby facility during off-hours.

### Akai, 26 — Turkana County

**Household:** De facto female-headed; four children; husband away herding livestock most of the year; Akai is provider through charcoal-selling and broom-making.

**Life arc:** Childhood marked by constant hunger; never attended school. Parents could not afford fees. Married at 14 when her family migrated and left her behind. Husband arrived for sex; she was terrified, closed her eyes and prayed. First pregnancy at 15 — did not recognise it until her belly grew. Delivered standing against a tree, holding on for support, with only a neighbour present. Has delivered four children; one was a self-delivery with a razor blade while alone, because her daughter was simultaneously critically ill and she could not leave. Lost one pregnancy to miscarriage after walking two hours to a water source in extreme heat — reached the clinic only after losing the baby. Access to food is charity-dependent: church food distributions are a survival resource. Water collection is a daily two-hour return journey.

**Key vulnerability drivers:** Childhood food insecurity → no education → very early marriage with no consent → unprepared sexual debut → absent husband → extreme geographic isolation from health services → climate-driven livelihood collapse → structural impossibility of consistent care-seeking.

**Mitigating factors:** Remarkable self-sufficiency and physical resilience. Community recognition of her competence (word spread about her self-delivery, which gave her status). Uncle provided financial support in emergency. Occasional church food relief.

**Friction points in care:** Clinic is too far for unplanned labour; water access itself is a health hazard; cannot read clinic documents given to her; husband absent and cannot be consulted; no economic buffer for health emergencies.

### Jane — Mathare, Nairobi *(from Pathways Kenya Life Journey documentation)*

**Household context:** Urban, female-headed; history of domestic violence, international labour migration, and serial structural shocks.

**Life arc summary:** Born in Mathare in 1983; raised by single mother; placed in a children's home at age 12 after family fragmentation. Left the home at 19, became a hairdresser and achieved financial independence. Experienced unexpected pregnancy at 20 (discovered at 8 months during appendix surgery); entered an abusive relationship; left to work in Saudi Arabia as a house helper where she was exploited by her employer. Returned to Mathare; experienced further domestic violence. At 35–36: a second unexpected pregnancy (while on family planning), fistula diagnosed 4 months after birth due to low quality of care, brothers killed in post-election violence, leg broken in an accident on the way to the clinic.

**Key insight from this journey:** Vulnerability accumulated through successive, overlapping shocks with no recovery period between them. Brief windows of resilience (financial independence as a hairdresser, high mobility at 19) were subsequently overwhelmed by structural forces (violence, migration, low-quality care, political insecurity). A single programme addressing any one of these factors would not have changed the trajectory.

## Cross-Cutting Patterns: Kenya

### 1. The Concept of Safe Birth Is Culturally Specific
Women across Kenya's most vulnerable segments have strong intentions to birth safely — but what "safe" means differs fundamentally. For rural pastoralist women, facility birth may feel *less* safe: it is far away, requires abandoning the 40-day rest practice, and involves providers who do not speak their language or understand their cultural protocols. Interventions must engage this cultural definition of safety, not override it.

### 2. Climate Is a Health Determinant
In Tana River and Turkana, climate shocks (drought reducing livestock herds, extreme heat causing pregnancy loss, water scarcity requiring dangerous daily journeys) are direct drivers of poor maternal and child health outcomes. Health programming in these contexts cannot be separated from climate adaptation and food security.

### 3. Urban Vulnerability Is Not Visible
Maggy's story exposes the invisibility of urban vulnerability. She is mobile, educated by local standards, and resourceful — but structurally trapped. The conditions of her vulnerability (informal work, absent social support, compounding caring responsibilities, economic razor's edge) do not match stereotypes of the "most vulnerable" and are easily missed by aggregate data.

### 4. Structural Barriers Are Not Behavioural Problems
Akai knows she should attend ANC. Amina would attend more often if she could. Maggy intended to deliver in a facility. Their non-attendance is not a behaviour to be changed through communication; it is a structural problem requiring structural solutions — outreach services, emergency transport, community-based care.

### 5. Traditional Birth Attendants Are Assets, Not Obstacles
In both Tana River (mkunga) and Turkana (neighbours and community women), traditional birth support systems are the *actual* care infrastructure available to these women. Effective interventions integrate and support these systems rather than bypassing them.

---

## Implications for Intervention in This Context

- **Outreach and mobile services** are not optional add-ons for rural Kenya; they are the only feasible primary care pathway for nomadic and semi-nomadic women.
- **Mkunga and TBA integration** into referral pathways is essential for safe delivery in remote contexts.
- **Address the 40-day postpartum rest** as a health asset and a community practice to be worked with (postnatal care can happen *within* this framework) not against.
- **Urban female-headed household programming** requires flexible scheduling, childcare integration, and mental health support.
- **Husband permission as a barrier** requires community-level male engagement — not individual negotiation by women at risk.
- **Climate-sensitive programming** must be the norm in arid and semi-arid contexts, not a specialist add-on.
