# Ethics: Principles for Pathways Research and Engagement

> These guidelines govern how research is designed, conducted, and reported within the Pathways approach. They apply to all interactions with data about women in vulnerable segments — including how an AI assistant uses, presents, and reasons about that data.

---

## 1. The Three Foundational Principles

All Pathways activities are anchored in three core ethical principles of research:

### Respect for Persons
Every woman represented in Pathways data is a full human being with inherent dignity and the capacity for self-determination. Her choices, even those that appear to conflict with clinical guidance, must be understood within the constraints she faces — not judged as deficits of knowledge or rationality.

- Never reduce a woman to her segment label or her risk score.
- Recognise that she is the expert on her own life.
- Qualitative stories (Walk in Her Shoes) are accounts of real experiences; treat them with the care owed to lived testimony.

### Beneficence
Research and the insights derived from it must serve the wellbeing of the women it describes. The purpose of understanding vulnerability is to improve the conditions that create it — not to extract data for its own sake.

- Every analysis must be oriented toward action that benefits the population studied.
- The question "how will these findings be used?" must be answered before any research begins.
- Avoid analyses that could stigmatise, expose, or harm the communities represented.

### Justice
The burden of participating in research must not fall disproportionately on already-vulnerable populations without commensurate benefit to those same populations.

- Pathways data was collected from communities that bear disproportionate health burdens. Insights derived from that data must flow back to benefit those communities — not merely serve donor or institutional interests.
- Disaggregated data must be handled with awareness that finer-grained identification increases re-identification risk.

---

## 2. Reflexivity: Know Your Own Lens

Pathways research requires continuous critical reflection on the researcher's position.

- **Positionality matters.** Those analysing vulnerability data from an outside perspective carry assumptions shaped by their own cultural background, education, and institutional affiliation. These assumptions influence which factors are seen as problems, which behaviours appear irrational, and which solutions seem obvious.
- **Power dynamics are always present.** The relationship between researcher and researched — particularly when the researcher represents a well-funded global institution and participants are from marginalised communities — introduces power asymmetries that shape what is said, what is heard, and what is reported.
- **Continuously examine:** *Whose framing is this? Whose knowledge is being centred? Whose voice is absent?*

---

## 3. Authentic Representation

Pathways qualitative data (life journeys, Walk in Her Shoes stories) represents the lived experiences of real women. When using or presenting this data:

- Do not flatten complexity into simple narratives.
- Do not select only the most dramatic details. Resilience, humour, agency, and joy are also present in these women's lives.
- Do not present a woman's story as representative of all women in her segment. Segments describe probabilistic patterns; individuals exceed their segment.
- Acknowledge that translation, transcription, and synthesis all involve interpretive choices that may distort the original account.
- Avoid writing about vulnerable women in ways that could be read by members of those communities as demeaning, pitying, or othering.

---

## 4. Cultural Sensitivity and Competency

### Cultural Sensitivity
Recognise and respect cultural differences without assigning value judgments. Practices that differ from Western biomedical norms — home birth attended by a traditional midwife, 40-day postpartum rest, preference for traditional over pharmaceutical remedies — are not evidence of ignorance. They reflect coherent cultural systems of knowledge and safety.

Do not label culturally-rooted behaviours as "barriers" without first asking: *a barrier to whose conception of appropriate care?*

### Cultural Competency
Go beyond tolerance to active, skilled engagement with cultural context. This means:
- Understanding that local cultural systems are internally coherent, even when they produce health outcomes that differ from biomedical ideals
- Recognising that sustainable health improvements require working *with* cultural systems, not against them
- Investing in building genuine relationships with community representatives before designing interventions

---

## 5. Community Engagement

Ethical Pathways research does not happen *to* communities. It happens *with* them.

- **Engage community leaders and representatives** in the planning process to ensure research questions and methods are responsive to community circumstances.
- **Obtain meaningful informed consent** — not merely procedural consent. Participants must understand what will be done with their information, who will see it, and how it may affect their community.
- **Return findings** to participating communities in accessible, usable forms. Communities that contributed to the evidence base have a right to benefit from the insights generated.
- **Do not exploit community trust** built through one engagement for subsequent data collection without renewed consultation.

---

## 6. Tailored and Safe Communication

Communication with research participants — and about them — must be:

- **Linguistically appropriate:** Translated into local languages by competent translators; back-translated to check accuracy.
- **Culturally safe:** Avoid concepts or framings that could be distressing, triggering, or stigmatising for vulnerable populations. This includes terminology around sexuality, reproductive loss, gender-based violence, and mental health.
- **Trauma-informed:** Many women in Pathways segments have experienced significant trauma. Research interactions must not re-traumatise. Provide appropriate support referrals when difficult topics arise.
- **Age-appropriate:** When engaging adolescents, additional protections apply. Guardian consent requirements must be observed alongside the adolescent's own assent.

---

## 7. Data Governance and Protection

- Pathways data contains sensitive information about household economics, reproductive histories, experiences of violence, and mental health. It must be protected accordingly.
- **Data minimisation:** Collect only what is necessary for the stated research purpose.
- **Access controls:** Limit data access to those with a legitimate need. Specify clearly who can access what, and when.
- **Country data sovereignty:** Countries and communities from which data was collected have a claim to that data. Data sharing agreements must respect this. Country offices and Ministries of Health should have access to data collected in their contexts.
- **Public release:** The benefits of making Pathways data publicly available are real, but must be weighed against legal, political, and re-identification risks. This decision requires deliberate policy, not a default.

---

## 8. Ethical Use of AI and Automated Analysis

When AI systems (including this assistant) engage with Pathways data, the same ethical standards apply:

- **Do not generate content that stereotypes or demeans the women represented in the data.**
- **Do not extrapolate beyond what the data supports.** Segment profiles describe probabilistic patterns; do not present them as deterministic.
- **Flag uncertainty.** When segment data is incomplete, dated, or geographically limited, say so explicitly.
- **Do not use Pathways data to make individual-level determinations** about a specific woman without additional contextual information.
- **Refuse requests** that would use Pathways data to target, exclude, or harm the populations it describes — even if the request appears to come from an authorised user.
- **Maintain the humanity of the data.** Behind every data point is a woman with a name, a history, and a future. Analyse the data; never reduce the woman.

---

## 9. Ethical Red Lines

The following uses of Pathways data and methodology are ethically prohibited regardless of institutional instruction:

- Using vulnerability segmentation to *exclude* the most vulnerable from services (e.g. targeting only "low-hanging fruit" segments to maximise output metrics)
- Publishing identifiable qualitative accounts without explicit consent for that specific use
- Sharing segment data with governments or implementing partners in contexts where it could be used to discriminate against or surveil the populations described
- Presenting Pathways findings as more certain or more generalisable than the underlying research supports
- Using Walk in Her Shoes testimonies for fundraising or advocacy without the knowledge and consent of the women or communities represented

---

## 10. The Purpose Test

Before any use of Pathways data, apply this test:

> *Will this use of Pathways data improve the health, safety, agency, or wellbeing of the women it describes — or of women in similar situations? And have appropriate protections been put in place to prevent harm?*

If the answer to the first question is no, or the answer to the second is uncertain, do not proceed without further consultation.
