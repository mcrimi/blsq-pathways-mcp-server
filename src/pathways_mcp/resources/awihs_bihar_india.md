# Walk in Her Shoes: Bihar, India

> This document synthesises the qualitative life stories collected in rural Bihar as part of the Pathways "Walk in Her Shoes" series. These accounts illuminate the *lived experience* of vulnerability — the texture of daily life that quantitative segmentation cannot fully capture. Use these stories to ground analysis in human reality, not just data points.

---

## Context

Bihar is one of India's poorest and most populous states. The women in this series live in rural districts — Darbhanga, Gaya, Siwan — in households shaped by:

- Deep patriarchal norms in which women's autonomy over marriage, reproduction, and health is severely constrained
- Male out-migration for work in distant cities (Delhi, Mumbai), leaving women to manage households alone while remaining economically dependent on irregular remittances
- Multi-generational joint family structures in which mothers-in-law hold significant authority over younger women's daily lives and health decisions
- Limited access to health facilities, particularly for those without vehicles or independent income
- Pervasive food insecurity, especially for women at the bottom of household food distribution hierarchies

## The Women

### Parvati, 26 — Darbhanga District

**Household:** Lives with four children and mother-in-law; husband migrant worker in Delhi, visits 1–2 months/year.

**Life arc:** Lost her mother at age 10; dropped out of school before completing primary grades; began doing paid domestic labour as a child. Married at 17 to a man of 29 with no income. Entered marriage with no preparation for sexual debut; husband made all decisions regarding sex and contraception. Experienced five pregnancies by age 21, including a neonatal death she blamed on her own weakness. Cannot access the clinic independently — it requires money for transport she does not have. Joined a savings group, saving ₹100/month, her only independent financial asset. Wants to use contraception but fears side effects and must hide it from her family.

**Key vulnerability drivers:** Childhood parental loss → education truncated → child labour → early marriage with large age gap → unprepared sexual debut → rapid successive pregnancies → absent partner → no transport to clinic → weak social network of equally impoverished neighbours.

**Mitigating factors:** Mother-in-law provides childcare support. Savings group membership. Emerging intention to use contraception.

**Friction points in care:** Economic barrier to clinic access; contraception use requires hiding from family; no phone to contact support services; social network too poor to provide material help.

### Rinky Kumari, 24 — Gaya District

**Household:** Lives with in-laws in a large household of 16 members including 8 children; husband migrant security guard in Mumbai, visits 2 months/year. Father-in-law is household head and sole decision-maker.

**Life arc:** Grew up poor with strong gender norms restricting mobility and education. Dropped out after primary school; teacher urged her to stay but "that was not my decision to make." Married at 16 to a man 8 years older. Entered marriage terrified and unprepared for sexual debut. Husband left for Mumbai shortly after marriage; has had minimal emotional relationship since. First child born underweight; second child also underweight despite years passing. Husband opposes contraception, believes it is harmful. She does not challenge this. Currently pregnant for the fourth time. Believes health decisions should be made by elders, not by her.

**Key vulnerability drivers:** Rigid gender norms from childhood → no education → early marriage → absent partner → no decision-making power → contraception blocked by husband → inaccurate health beliefs reinforced by mother-in-law → repeated poor birth outcomes.

**Mitigating factors:** Has ASHA contact in the village. Some family support structure (large household, though also a source of pressure).

**Friction points in care:** All health decisions deferred to husband or elders; clinic visits only happen when she collapses, not preventatively; distrust of hospital interventions (fears surgery); no independent communications link to husband.

### Malti, 22 — Siwan District

**Household:** Lives with large agricultural joint family (14 members); husband runs appliance repair shop in the village. No women need to work outside the home.

**Life arc:** Had a relatively supportive childhood — father encouraged her education through high school, against grandmother's wishes. Got to first year of college before marriage at 19. Husband is supportive and bought her a phone; they communicate well. However, mother-in-law holds day-to-day authority over household decisions, including child health. Wanted to vaccinate daughter on schedule; mother-in-law blocked it until ASHA intervened (after which mother-in-law turned hostile toward the ASHA). When daughter fell ill and husband was away, Malti waited two days before taking her to hospital because she "could not find any time" — and because it was not considered her decision to make alone. Opposes hormonal contraception (misinformation about "chemical things"); second pregnancy already underway.

**Key vulnerability drivers:** Despite relative assets and education, *relational constraint* is the primary barrier. Mother-in-law gatekeeping overrides both her knowledge and her intentions. Low independent decision-making power even in a comparatively advantaged household.

**Mitigating factors:** Higher education than most women in these stories. Supportive, present husband who can be "convinced to convince his mother." Relationship with eldest sister-in-law as trusted confidante. Access to a phone and information.

**Friction points in care:** Misinformation about contraception (not corrected by husband); all significant decisions require male intermediary; workload and childcare burden means health crises go unaddressed for days; uncertain whether she can go to parental home for delivery (not her decision to make).
